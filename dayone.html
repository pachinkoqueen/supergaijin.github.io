Day One

Prepare to learn

Checklist

At the end of this course, you will have conducted meaningful 30-45 minute conversations with around 8-10 carefully selected users.

Preparation for research revolves around three elements:

Prototype

What you will show

Questions

What you want to learn

Recruits

Who you will ask

UX Research is best conducted as a group activity; make sure your core team is involved during this phase. Facilitate the conversation and take notes and allow the team to focus on what they want to learn. It may not be a linear process, as one element may influence the other two. Likely you will bounce between all three and make refinements as you go.

Draft Script

Start by deciding what you will put in front of users. Using the term “prototype” as a loose definition, this might be your live site or something you've been working on. Format can vary from code to PDF to Sketch artboards. The level of fidelity is up to you; just make sure you won't spend the majority of the interview explaining the prototype.

Because interviews will be roughly 30 minutes, respect your users' time by presenting just enough. You want users to accomplish a task and a single screen generally will not give enough context. Consider defining a user task as a starting point.

“Can I show several versions of a screen and have users vote?”
Avoid this scenario for a few reasons. Having users review isolated screens removes the context of use and takes them into a passive "Mona Lisa Mode" in which screens are observed like paintings instead of a tool to accomplish a task. Additionally, you will not be talking to a sample size large enough for voting responses to be meaningful.

Key Discoverables

Define your learning objectives and why you're testing in the first place. Perhaps you want to understand where the drop-off points are in your onboarding experience. Or reactions to a newly-redesigned feature. Assemble your questions into a document which will serve as your script.

Example

We want to evaluate the user flow and visualizations in our new retirement planning feature.

Specific Questions

Walk through the prototype and document questions on each screen. Don't be concerned with having to answer every single question; because you are creating a habit of regular research you will answer these in future sessions.

Example

“Can the user find the retirement planning tool from the home page?”

“Does the user understand what happens when they click the 'Calculate my retirement' button?”

Discovery Questions

You'll want gauge a users' ability to use your product; but even a product that is easy to use doesn't mean a user will engage with it - perhaps because they've got their own tools and processes that work for them. For this reason, it is helpful to tack on discovery questions at the end to better understand how your users think about a particular task, or their attitudes and beliefs.

Example

  • “Where do you usually go to seek retirement savings advice? Why?”
  • “If I asked you to describe your outlook when it comes to the amount you have saved what would you say? Why?”

Draft Script: Complete

Write Screener

The best possible audience is your actual users. If your users have a login and can be identified through usage data, even better.

Who do we want to talk to?

Determine the characteristics of the users you want to talk to. As you take notes, determine specifically how you will get at this information. Put them in your draft script for now, and enter them into ethn.io later.

Example

We want to evaluate the user flow and visualizations in our new retirement planning feature.

We want to talk to

  • Active users who have logged into our app in the past 2 months [get from analytics]
  • Mix of early retirement savers (20's 30's) and later-stage retirement savers (40's and 50's) [age]

Who do we NOT want to talk to?

Just as important as who you are talking to, determine who you do not want to talk to, such as users with special expertise or knowledge, or are unengaged in the activity you are studying.

Example

We want to evaluate the user flow and visualizations in our new retirement planning feature.

We do not want to talk to

  • Dormant users who have not logged into the app in the past 2 weeks [get from analytics]
  • Financial planners, anyone involved in the finance industry [job title]
  • Retirees [job title] OR [are you retired? Y/N]
  • Anyone not actively saving for retirement [are you currently saving for retirement? Y/N]
    [better: which of the retirement accounts do you have 401K/Roth IRA/Health Savings Account(HSA)/Other(what)]

Make sure your screener questions are clear to avoid false positives and negatives

Example

Subject to interpretation
Are you on a High Deductible Healthcare Plan (HDHP)? 
“Yes, I think my healthcare deductible is way too high.”

Clearer version
Are you on a High Deductible Healthcare Plan (HDHP) with an annual deductible of $1250 or higher? 
“Oh. No.”

Remember it's not a survey

Demographic data such as gender, marital status, household income is only helpful to the extent it affects the behavior you are researching. For example, in researching a retirement planning tool, someone who is 26 may view retirement savings differently than a 50 year old. Remember, you aren't conducting a quantitative survey. You are only going to interview 8-10 users. Keep in mind you are developing a habit of talking to users and you will have many more opportunities to further your research.

Write Screener: Complete

Define Recruit Pool

Your recruit pool is comprised of the users who will get an invite. To recruit 8-10 users, aim to send 50-80 emails out. Adjust accordingly based on responses. Avoid sending out a blanket email to all your users. The goal is to build a habit of talking to users regularly and if you keep sending out emails to your entire user base, you'll likely annoy them.

FUNNEL Step one: Reduce recruits based on analytics data (user pool > analytics requirements) Step two: Randomize Step three: Send the screener

If possible, separate usage data you can get from analytics from screener questions users will answer. This allows you to whittle down your pool and reduces user error answering screener questions - you don't have to rely on a user trying to recall the last time they logged in. Use SQL to filter out or pinpoint users; or find someone who will teach you how.

Example

  • Users who have logged at least once in the past 2 weeks
  • Users who have downloaded our mobile app

Define Recruit Pool: Complete

Send Screener

Now it's time to send out your screener. Use an email marketing app if you have access to one; MailChimp is a good one. For subject lines, I have found a simple “Can you help?” performs well. For the body content, keep it short and sweet.

Example

$20 Amazon gift card for 30 minutes of your time
We are looking for feedback on new app features . All you need is a computer and an internet connection. [link to screener]

Mondays are typically low turnout. Aim for Wednesdays, Thursdays, and Friday mornings. Be mindful of time zones and communicate clearly to your users what time zone you are in.

Send Out Screener: Complete

Common Questions

“Isn't it easier to send a survey?”
Nope. Surveys are not a good tool to understand user behavior on a web site for the following reasons: Surveys are a quantitative tool Good survey questions are difficult to write Asking users to recall browsing behavior will likely result in incorrect self-reporting You miss opportunities to ask valuable of-the-moment questions.

“What if I can't talk to real users?”
If you find yourself in a position where you cannot talk to users with logins, there are still options available. You can recruit on Craigslist, Facebook, Twitter, Task Rabbit, or online classifieds. Be sure to screen carefully.

“Should we talk to our friends and family?”
Be cautious of using a friends and family pool, as it is far too easy for internal audiences to dismiss your results. You can recruit within your personal network, as long as they meet screening requirements. My rule of thumb is FOAF ("Friend of a Friend"); I do not want to have ever met the person I am talking to. DO not rely heavily on this pool.

“Should we test with a regular panel?”
No. Relying on a small set of users to represent all users will cut you off from potentially valuable learnings. Additionally, test panels have the tendency to know too much about the product and will become too much a part of the organization. Keep your user pool fresh and you will evolve your learnings over time.
